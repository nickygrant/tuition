## Courses taught

### ECON30401 — Time Series Econometrics (University of Manchester, 3rd year undergraduate)
Dr Grant taught Lectures 1–4 (univariate time series). Lectures 5–8 by Prof. Alastair R. Hall (VAR, unit roots).

**Lecture 1: Introduction to Time Series Econometrics**
Topics: Time series process vs. realisation. Stationarity: covariance-stationarity requires E[Yt] = mu for all t, Var[Yt] = sigma^2 < infinity for all t, Cov(Yt, Yt-k) = gamma(k) depending only on k. White noise: E[et] = 0, Var[et] = sigma^2, Cov(et, et-k) = 0 for k≠0. MA(q) process: Yt = alpha + sum_{j=0}^{q} theta_j * e_{t-j}. ACF of MA(q) cuts off after lag q.

**Lecture 2: ARMA(p,q) Processes & Stationarity**
Topics: MA(infinity) process. Squared summability condition: sum_{s=0}^{infinity} theta_s^2 < infinity. Absolute summability: sum_{s=0}^{infinity} |theta_s| < infinity. Wold Decomposition Theorem: any stationary process can be represented as MA(infinity). AR(1) as MA(infinity): when |phi_1| < 1, Yt = alpha/(1-phi_1) + sum_{j=0}^{infinity} phi_1^j * e_{t-j}. AR(1) mean: mu = alpha/(1-phi_1). AR(1) variance: sigma^2/(1-phi_1^2). AR(1) ACF: rho(k) = phi_1^k. ARMA(1,1): Yt = mu + phi_1*Y_{t-1} + theta_1*e_{t-1} + e_t, stationary when |phi_1| < 1. ARMA(p,q): general class modelling complex dynamics. Stationarity condition via characteristic polynomial: roots outside unit circle.

**Lecture 3: Estimation, Hypothesis Testing & Model Selection**
Topics: Sample ACF: rho_hat(k) = sum_{t=k+1}^{T}(Yt - Ybar)(Y_{t-k} - Ybar) / sum_{t=1}^{T}(Yt - Ybar)^2. Under H0: rho(k)=0, sqrt(T)*rho_hat(k) ~ N(0,1) asymptotically. 95% confidence bands: ±1.96/sqrt(T). Ljung-Box portmanteau test: Q(m) = T(T+2) * sum_{k=1}^{m} rho_hat(k)^2/(T-k) ~ chi^2(m). AR estimation by OLS. MA and ARMA by MLE. AIC = -2*log(L) + 2*K. BIC = -2*log(L) + K*log(T). Application to UK GDP growth data.

**Lecture 4: Prediction & Seasonality**
Topics: AR(1) 1-step prediction: Y_hat_{T+1} = mu + phi_1*y_T. J-step prediction: Y_hat_{T+J} = mu + phi_1*Y_hat_{T+J-1}. Forecast error: Y_{T+J} - Y_hat_{T+J} = sum_{j=1}^{J} phi_1^{J-j} * e_{T+j}. Forecast error variance: sigma^2 * sum_{j=1}^{J} phi_1^{2(J-j)}. As J→infinity: Y_hat_{T+J} → mu/(1-phi_1), variance → sigma^2/(1-phi_1^2). Deterministic seasonality: seasonal dummy variables. Stochastic seasonality: seasonal AR(1) SAR(1): Yt = alpha + phi_4*Y_{t-4} + e_t, stationary if |phi_4| < 1.

**Week 1-2 Notes** (comprehensive written notes): Full derivations of all lecture 1-2 material. Available free: drnickygrant.com/teaching/ts-notes-weeks1-2.pdf

### ECON61001 — Econometric Methods (University of Manchester, postgraduate)
Dr Grant taught Lectures 5–7.

**Lecture 5: Diagnostic Testing & Robust Inference**
OLS assumptions: (A1) linearity, (A2) exogeneity E[ui|X]=0, (A3) homoskedasticity E[ui^2|X]=sigma^2, (A4) no serial correlation E[ui*uj|X]=0, (A5) regularity. Testing A3 (heteroskedasticity): White test — regress u_hat^2 on xi, xi^2, xi*xj; T*R^2 ~ chi^2(p) under H0. Breusch-Pagan test. HC (heteroskedasticity-consistent) standard errors. Testing A4 (serial correlation): Durbin-Watson statistic. Breusch-Godfrey LM test: regress residuals on X and lagged residuals; T*R^2 ~ chi^2(p). HAC (Newey-West) standard errors.

**Lecture 6: Endogeneity & Instrumental Variables (2SLS)**
Endogeneity: E[ui*xi] ≠ 0 — OLS biased and inconsistent. Sources: simultaneity, omitted variables, measurement error. IV estimator: beta_IV = (Z'X)^{-1}Z'y where Z is the matrix of instruments. Valid instrument conditions: (1) Relevance: Cov(zi, xi) ≠ 0. (2) Exclusion restriction: Cov(zi, ui) = 0. 2SLS: first stage — regress xi on zi; second stage — regress yi on x_hat_i. First stage F-statistic (F > 10 as rule of thumb). Application: Acemoglu-Johnson-Robinson settler mortality instrument for institutional quality.

**Lecture 7: Weak & Unidentified Instruments**
Weak instrument problem: when Cov(zi, xi) ≈ 0, 2SLS is severely biased toward OLS; standard inference invalid. Concentration parameter mu^2 = (pi^2 * Var(zi)) / Var(vi). Stock-Yogo (2005) critical values: for 5% maximal IV size distortion with 1 instrument, first-stage F > 16.38. Anderson-Rubin (AR) test: AR = (y - X*b)' Pz (y - X*b) / [(y - X*b)' Mz (y - X*b) / (T-k)] ~ F(l, T-k) under H0. LIML (Limited Information Maximum Likelihood): less biased than 2SLS, invariant to normalization. Identification-robust inference: conditional likelihood ratio (CLR) test.

### EC5609 — Financial Econometrics (University of St Andrews, postgraduate MSc)
Full course taught by Dr Grant, Semester 1.

**Week 1: Distribution Theory and Properties of Stock Price Returns**
Topics: Financial econometric data. Stock price returns: R_t = (P_t - P_{t-1})/P_{t-1} or log-returns r_t = log(P_t) - log(P_{t-1}). Statistical distribution theory: PDF, CDF, moments. Mean, variance, skewness (third standardised central moment), kurtosis (fourth standardised central moment). Leptokurtosis in financial returns: excess kurtosis > 0. Fat tails. Normal vs. Student-t distribution for returns. Sampling and moment estimation. Central Limit Theorem and asymptotic normality of sample moments.

**Lecture 7: Advanced Volatility Modelling — GARCH**
Topics: Conditional heteroskedasticity. ARCH(q): h_t = omega + sum_{i=1}^{q} alpha_i * e_{t-i}^2. GARCH(1,1): h_t = omega + alpha * e_{t-1}^2 + beta * h_{t-1}, with omega>0, alpha>=0, beta>=0, alpha+beta<1 for stationarity. Volatility clustering. Asymmetric volatility (leverage effect): negative returns increase volatility more than positive returns. EGARCH (Nelson): log(h_t) = omega + alpha*(|e_{t-1}|/sqrt(h_{t-1}) - sqrt(2/pi)) + gamma*(e_{t-1}/sqrt(h_{t-1})) + beta*log(h_{t-1}). TARCH/GJR-GARCH. GARCH-in-mean. Estimation by MLE. Generalised error distribution (GED) for fat tails.

**Maximum Likelihood in Financial Econometrics**
MLE: maximise log-likelihood L(theta) = sum log f(yi; theta). For normal returns: L = -T/2*log(2*pi*sigma^2) - sum(yi-mu)^2/(2*sigma^2). For GARCH: conditional log-likelihood with time-varying h_t.

## Oxbridge Economics Interview — Question Types and Worked Solutions

Published: 2026-06-14
URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/oxbridge-economics-interview-questions-worked.html

Cambridge and Oxford economics interviews assess five distinct question types. Full worked solutions:

### 1. Open-ended economic reasoning
Example: "Should a good economic model be able to predict economic outcomes?"
Approach: Structure the question by purpose of model. Cases where prediction is central (policy analysis, fiscal multipliers). Cases where prediction is neither possible nor the goal (stock prices under EMH, models used for conceptual clarity). Key concepts: Friedman's instrumentalism (1953 essay), Lucas critique (rational agents change behaviour in response to predicted policy, invalidating the model), Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"), Diamond-Dybvig bank run model (multiple equilibria — identifies fragility without predicting timing).
Core message: "A good economic model should generate testable predictions where the purpose requires it. Policy models should be judged on predictive accuracy. Models for conceptual clarity or welfare analysis need not."

### 2. Technical economics: price discrimination
1st degree (perfect): firm charges each consumer exactly their reservation price (WTP). Consumer surplus = 0. Efficient (no DWL) because every transaction with WTP ≥ MC occurs. Producer captures full surplus. Information requirement: individual WTP — infeasible in practice; approximated by negotiation or personalised algorithmic pricing.
3rd degree: different prices to different groups segmented by observable characteristic (student status, geography, time). Condition: groups must have different price elasticities. Profit-maximising rule: MR₁ = MR₂ = MC. Using MR = p(1 - 1/|ε|): inelastic group (lower |ε|) pays higher price. Examples: student software discounts, peak vs off-peak rail, pharmaceutical pricing by country. May create DWL in elastic segment if output is restricted.
Key distinctions: 1st degree individual prices vs 3rd degree group prices; 1st degree efficient vs 3rd degree potentially DWL; 1st degree full extraction vs 3rd degree partial extraction.

### 3. Applied economics / game theory: Battle of the Sexes
Matrix: (O,O)=(2,1), (O,F)=(0,0), (F,O)=(0,0), (F,F)=(1,2) where payoffs are (A,B).
Pure Nash Equilibria: (O,O) and (F,F). Both coordination equilibria — players prefer coordinating on any pure NE over no coordination, but disagree about which.
Mixed strategy NE: p* = 2/3 (A plays O), q* = 1/3 (B plays O). Expected payoffs in mixed NE = 2/3 for both (strictly worse than either pure NE).
Coordination problem: equilibrium analysis alone cannot resolve which NE is played. Focal points (Schelling), commitment devices, mediators, or repeated game conventions required.
Real-world examples: technology standard coordination (VHS vs Betamax), international treaty adoption, platform competition.

### 4. Probability / statistics: conditional probability (two coins)
Two coins: P(H|coin A) = 1/4, P(H|coin B) = 3/4. Pick at random. Observe Heads. What is P(same coin shows Heads again)?
Solution via Bayes' theorem:
Step 1: P(H₁) = (1/4)(1/2) + (3/4)(1/2) = 1/2
Step 2: P(A|H₁) = (1/8)/(1/2) = 1/4; P(B|H₁) = (3/8)/(1/2) = 3/4
Step 3: P(H₂|H₁) = (1/4)(1/4) + (3/4)(3/4) = 1/16 + 9/16 = 10/16 = 5/8
Answer: 5/8. Key principle: Bayesian updating — the first throw is evidence about which coin is held; update beliefs before computing next-throw probability.

### 5. Logic / problem solving: investment strategy (gambler's ruin variant)
Investor starts with ¥10,000. Each round bets ¥10,000: 50% win ¥10,000, 50% lose ¥10,000. Plays until bankrupt. P(wealth ≥ ¥10,000 after 3 rounds)?
All paths:
- Loss (R1): p=1/2 → ¥0 ✗
- Win, Win, Win: p=1/8 → ¥40,000 ✓
- Win, Win, Loss: p=1/8 → ¥20,000 ✓
- Win, Loss, Win: p=1/8 → ¥20,000 ✓
- Win, Loss, Loss: p=1/8 → ¥0 ✗
P(≥ ¥10,000) = 3/8. Key insight: expected value per round is zero, but P(at least break even) < 1/2 due to absorbing barrier at ¥0. Volatility and ruin risk matter independently of mean — relevance to financial economics, Kelly criterion.

## Economics Personal Statement Guide

Published: 2026-06-14
URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/economics-personal-statement-guide.html

### Core principle
The gap between a strong and weak economics personal statement is not effort but a misunderstanding of what admissions tutors evaluate. Strong statements demonstrate doing economics (specific projects, methods, findings). Weak statements demonstrate thinking about economics (book summaries, economic observations, general assertions of interest).

### Three-section structure evaluated
Q1 — Why this subject? Must be direct, specific, intellectually grounded. Should state: specific moment/observation → economic concept it connects to → unanswered question → why this degree is the tool.
Q2 — Academic preparation: for every activity mentioned, must state: specific task/project, specific method or technique used, specific data source, specific explicit finding. "I used statistical analysis" is not informative. "I estimated an OLS regression of Y on X using OECD data and found..." is.
Q3 — Outside school: real activities with analytical content > passive reading. Reading only earns inclusion if it raises a specific unresolved question, not if it simply deepened interest.

### Technical evidence
Economics at Cambridge/Oxford is mathematically intensive. Personal statements must provide quantitative evidence: named statistical methods (OLS regression, White test, heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors), named software (STATA, R, Python), specific data sources. Do not substitute vague terms ("data analysis", "statistical techniques") for the specific method used.

### Books and podcasts
Include only if: the book/podcast raised a specific intellectual question the applicant wants to answer; the applicant can articulate why the question requires economics to address; and there is a connection to their own analytical work. Do not use as a reading list or status signal.

### Closing paragraph
Must be specific enough to apply only to this applicant. State the intellectual through-line connecting all the statement's activities and interests. Avoid: "I would make the most of the opportunity"; "I believe I have a lot to contribute"; generic "passion for economics."
