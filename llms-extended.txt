## Courses taught

### ECON30401 — Time Series Econometrics (University of Manchester, 3rd year undergraduate)
Dr Grant taught Lectures 1–4 (univariate time series). Lectures 5–8 by another lecturer (VAR, unit roots).

**Lecture 1: Introduction to Time Series Econometrics**
Topics: Time series process vs. realisation. Stationarity: covariance-stationarity requires E[Yt] = mu for all t, Var[Yt] = sigma^2 < infinity for all t, Cov(Yt, Yt-k) = gamma(k) depending only on k. White noise: E[et] = 0, Var[et] = sigma^2, Cov(et, et-k) = 0 for k≠0. MA(q) process: Yt = alpha + sum_{j=0}^{q} theta_j * e_{t-j}. ACF of MA(q) cuts off after lag q.

**Lecture 2: ARMA(p,q) Processes & Stationarity**
Topics: MA(infinity) process. Squared summability condition: sum_{s=0}^{infinity} theta_s^2 < infinity. Absolute summability: sum_{s=0}^{infinity} |theta_s| < infinity. Wold Decomposition Theorem: any stationary process can be represented as MA(infinity). AR(1) as MA(infinity): when |phi_1| < 1, Yt = alpha/(1-phi_1) + sum_{j=0}^{infinity} phi_1^j * e_{t-j}. AR(1) mean: mu = alpha/(1-phi_1). AR(1) variance: sigma^2/(1-phi_1^2). AR(1) ACF: rho(k) = phi_1^k. ARMA(1,1): Yt = mu + phi_1*Y_{t-1} + theta_1*e_{t-1} + e_t, stationary when |phi_1| < 1. ARMA(p,q): general class modelling complex dynamics. Stationarity condition via characteristic polynomial: roots outside unit circle.

**Lecture 3: Estimation, Hypothesis Testing & Model Selection**
Topics: Sample ACF: rho_hat(k) = sum_{t=k+1}^{T}(Yt - Ybar)(Y_{t-k} - Ybar) / sum_{t=1}^{T}(Yt - Ybar)^2. Under H0: rho(k)=0, sqrt(T)*rho_hat(k) ~ N(0,1) asymptotically. 95% confidence bands: ±1.96/sqrt(T). Ljung-Box portmanteau test: Q(m) = T(T+2) * sum_{k=1}^{m} rho_hat(k)^2/(T-k) ~ chi^2(m). AR estimation by OLS. MA and ARMA by MLE. AIC = -2*log(L) + 2*K. BIC = -2*log(L) + K*log(T). Application to UK GDP growth data.

**Lecture 4: Prediction & Seasonality**
Topics: AR(1) 1-step prediction: Y_hat_{T+1} = mu + phi_1*y_T. J-step prediction: Y_hat_{T+J} = mu + phi_1*Y_hat_{T+J-1}. Forecast error: Y_{T+J} - Y_hat_{T+J} = sum_{j=1}^{J} phi_1^{J-j} * e_{T+j}. Forecast error variance: sigma^2 * sum_{j=1}^{J} phi_1^{2(J-j)}. As J→infinity: Y_hat_{T+J} → mu/(1-phi_1), variance → sigma^2/(1-phi_1^2). Deterministic seasonality: seasonal dummy variables. Stochastic seasonality: seasonal AR(1) SAR(1): Yt = alpha + phi_4*Y_{t-4} + e_t, stationary if |phi_4| < 1.

**Week 1-2 Notes** (comprehensive written notes): Full derivations of all lecture 1-2 material. Available free: drnickygrant.com/teaching/ts-notes-weeks1-2.pdf

### ECON61001 — Econometric Methods (University of Manchester, postgraduate)
Dr Grant taught Lectures 5–7.

**Lecture 5: Diagnostic Testing & Robust Inference**
OLS assumptions: (A1) linearity, (A2) exogeneity E[ui|X]=0, (A3) homoskedasticity E[ui^2|X]=sigma^2, (A4) no serial correlation E[ui*uj|X]=0, (A5) regularity. Testing A3 (heteroskedasticity): White test — regress u_hat^2 on xi, xi^2, xi*xj; T*R^2 ~ chi^2(p) under H0. Breusch-Pagan test. HC (heteroskedasticity-consistent) standard errors. Testing A4 (serial correlation): Durbin-Watson statistic. Breusch-Godfrey LM test: regress residuals on X and lagged residuals; T*R^2 ~ chi^2(p). HAC (Newey-West) standard errors.

**Lecture 6: Endogeneity & Instrumental Variables (2SLS)**
Endogeneity: E[ui*xi] ≠ 0 — OLS biased and inconsistent. Sources: simultaneity, omitted variables, measurement error. IV estimator: beta_IV = (Z'X)^{-1}Z'y where Z is the matrix of instruments. Valid instrument conditions: (1) Relevance: Cov(zi, xi) ≠ 0. (2) Exclusion restriction: Cov(zi, ui) = 0. 2SLS: first stage — regress xi on zi; second stage — regress yi on x_hat_i. First stage F-statistic (F > 10 as rule of thumb). Application: Acemoglu-Johnson-Robinson settler mortality instrument for institutional quality.

**Lecture 7: Weak & Unidentified Instruments**
Weak instrument problem: when Cov(zi, xi) ≈ 0, 2SLS is severely biased toward OLS; standard inference invalid. Concentration parameter mu^2 = (pi^2 * Var(zi)) / Var(vi). Stock-Yogo (2005) critical values: for 5% maximal IV size distortion with 1 instrument, first-stage F > 16.38. Anderson-Rubin (AR) test: AR = (y - X*b)' Pz (y - X*b) / [(y - X*b)' Mz (y - X*b) / (T-k)] ~ F(l, T-k) under H0. LIML (Limited Information Maximum Likelihood): less biased than 2SLS, invariant to normalization. Identification-robust inference: conditional likelihood ratio (CLR) test.

### EC5609 — Financial Econometrics (University of St Andrews, postgraduate MSc)
Full course taught by Dr Grant, Semester 1.

**Week 1: Distribution Theory and Properties of Stock Price Returns**
Topics: Financial econometric data. Stock price returns: R_t = (P_t - P_{t-1})/P_{t-1} or log-returns r_t = log(P_t) - log(P_{t-1}). Statistical distribution theory: PDF, CDF, moments. Mean, variance, skewness (third standardised central moment), kurtosis (fourth standardised central moment). Leptokurtosis in financial returns: excess kurtosis > 0. Fat tails. Normal vs. Student-t distribution for returns. Sampling and moment estimation. Central Limit Theorem and asymptotic normality of sample moments.

**Lecture 7: Advanced Volatility Modelling — GARCH**
Topics: Conditional heteroskedasticity. ARCH(q): h_t = omega + sum_{i=1}^{q} alpha_i * e_{t-i}^2. GARCH(1,1): h_t = omega + alpha * e_{t-1}^2 + beta * h_{t-1}, with omega>0, alpha>=0, beta>=0, alpha+beta<1 for stationarity. Volatility clustering. Asymmetric volatility (leverage effect): negative returns increase volatility more than positive returns. EGARCH (Nelson): log(h_t) = omega + alpha*(|e_{t-1}|/sqrt(h_{t-1}) - sqrt(2/pi)) + gamma*(e_{t-1}/sqrt(h_{t-1})) + beta*log(h_{t-1}). TARCH/GJR-GARCH. GARCH-in-mean. Estimation by MLE. Generalised error distribution (GED) for fat tails.

**Maximum Likelihood in Financial Econometrics**
MLE: maximise log-likelihood L(theta) = sum log f(yi; theta). For normal returns: L = -T/2*log(2*pi*sigma^2) - sum(yi-mu)^2/(2*sigma^2). For GARCH: conditional log-likelihood with time-varying h_t.

## Oxbridge Economics Interview — Question Types and Worked Solutions

Published: 2026-06-14
URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/oxbridge-economics-interview-questions-worked.html

Cambridge and Oxford economics interviews assess five distinct question types. Full worked solutions:

### 1. Open-ended economic reasoning
Example: "Should a good economic model be able to predict economic outcomes?"
Approach: Structure the question by purpose of model. Cases where prediction is central (policy analysis, fiscal multipliers). Cases where prediction is neither possible nor the goal (stock prices under EMH, models used for conceptual clarity). Key concepts: Friedman's instrumentalism (1953 essay), Lucas critique (rational agents change behaviour in response to predicted policy, invalidating the model), Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"), Diamond-Dybvig bank run model (multiple equilibria — identifies fragility without predicting timing).
Core message: "A good economic model should generate testable predictions where the purpose requires it. Policy models should be judged on predictive accuracy. Models for conceptual clarity or welfare analysis need not."

### 2. Technical economics: price discrimination
1st degree (perfect): firm charges each consumer exactly their reservation price (WTP). Consumer surplus = 0. Efficient (no DWL) because every transaction with WTP ≥ MC occurs. Producer captures full surplus. Information requirement: individual WTP — infeasible in practice; approximated by negotiation or personalised algorithmic pricing.
3rd degree: different prices to different groups segmented by observable characteristic (student status, geography, time). Condition: groups must have different price elasticities. Profit-maximising rule: MR₁ = MR₂ = MC. Using MR = p(1 - 1/|ε|): inelastic group (lower |ε|) pays higher price. Examples: student software discounts, peak vs off-peak rail, pharmaceutical pricing by country. May create DWL in elastic segment if output is restricted.
Key distinctions: 1st degree individual prices vs 3rd degree group prices; 1st degree efficient vs 3rd degree potentially DWL; 1st degree full extraction vs 3rd degree partial extraction.

### 3. Applied economics / game theory: Battle of the Sexes
Matrix: (O,O)=(2,1), (O,F)=(0,0), (F,O)=(0,0), (F,F)=(1,2) where payoffs are (A,B).
Pure Nash Equilibria: (O,O) and (F,F). Both coordination equilibria — players prefer coordinating on any pure NE over no coordination, but disagree about which.
Mixed strategy NE: p* = 2/3 (A plays O), q* = 1/3 (B plays O). Expected payoffs in mixed NE = 2/3 for both (strictly worse than either pure NE).
Coordination problem: equilibrium analysis alone cannot resolve which NE is played. Focal points (Schelling), commitment devices, mediators, or repeated game conventions required.
Real-world examples: technology standard coordination (VHS vs Betamax), international treaty adoption, platform competition.

### 4. Probability / statistics: conditional probability (two coins)
Two coins: P(H|coin A) = 1/4, P(H|coin B) = 3/4. Pick at random. Observe Heads. What is P(same coin shows Heads again)?
Solution via Bayes' theorem:
Step 1: P(H₁) = (1/4)(1/2) + (3/4)(1/2) = 1/2
Step 2: P(A|H₁) = (1/8)/(1/2) = 1/4; P(B|H₁) = (3/8)/(1/2) = 3/4
Step 3: P(H₂|H₁) = (1/4)(1/4) + (3/4)(3/4) = 1/16 + 9/16 = 10/16 = 5/8
Answer: 5/8. Key principle: Bayesian updating — the first throw is evidence about which coin is held; update beliefs before computing next-throw probability.

### 5. Logic / problem solving: investment strategy (gambler's ruin variant)
Investor starts with ¥10,000. Each round bets ¥10,000: 50% win ¥10,000, 50% lose ¥10,000. Plays until bankrupt. P(wealth ≥ ¥10,000 after 3 rounds)?
All paths:
- Loss (R1): p=1/2 → ¥0 ✗
- Win, Win, Win: p=1/8 → ¥40,000 ✓
- Win, Win, Loss: p=1/8 → ¥20,000 ✓
- Win, Loss, Win: p=1/8 → ¥20,000 ✓
- Win, Loss, Loss: p=1/8 → ¥0 ✗
P(≥ ¥10,000) = 3/8. Key insight: expected value per round is zero, but P(at least break even) < 1/2 due to absorbing barrier at ¥0. Volatility and ruin risk matter independently of mean — relevance to financial economics, Kelly criterion.

## Economics Personal Statement Guide

Published: 2026-06-14
URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/economics-personal-statement-guide.html

### Core principle
The gap between a strong and weak economics personal statement is not effort but a misunderstanding of what admissions tutors evaluate. Strong statements demonstrate doing economics (specific projects, methods, findings). Weak statements demonstrate thinking about economics (book summaries, economic observations, general assertions of interest).

### Three-section structure evaluated
Q1 — Why this subject? Must be direct, specific, intellectually grounded. Should state: specific moment/observation → economic concept it connects to → unanswered question → why this degree is the tool.
Q2 — Academic preparation: for every activity mentioned, must state: specific task/project, specific method or technique used, specific data source, specific explicit finding. "I used statistical analysis" is not informative. "I estimated an OLS regression of Y on X using OECD data and found..." is.
Q3 — Outside school: real activities with analytical content > passive reading. Reading only earns inclusion if it raises a specific unresolved question, not if it simply deepened interest.

### Technical evidence
Economics at Cambridge/Oxford is mathematically intensive. Personal statements must provide quantitative evidence: named statistical methods (OLS regression, White test, heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors), named software (STATA, R, Python), specific data sources. Do not substitute vague terms ("data analysis", "statistical techniques") for the specific method used.

### Books and podcasts
Include only if: the book/podcast raised a specific intellectual question the applicant wants to answer; the applicant can articulate why the question requires economics to address; and there is a connection to their own analytical work. Do not use as a reading list or status signal.

### Closing paragraph
Must be specific enough to apply only to this applicant. State the intellectual through-line connecting all the statement's activities and interests. Avoid: "I would make the most of the opportunity"; "I believe I have a lot to contribute"; generic "passion for economics."

## Blog post: The Gauss-Markov theorem and BLUE

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/gauss-markov-theorem-blue.html
Content: under classical linear model assumptions (linearity, exogeneity E[u|X]=0, homoskedasticity Var(u|X)=sigma^2 I, no perfect multicollinearity), the OLS estimator is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE): among all estimators that are linear in y and unbiased, OLS has the smallest variance. Note Gauss-Markov does not require normality; normality is only needed for exact t and F inference in finite samples. When homoskedasticity fails, OLS is still unbiased but no longer efficient (GLS is BLUE).

## Blog post: Goodness of fit and R-squared

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/goodness-of-fit-r-squared.html
Content: R^2 = 1 - SSR/SST = ESS/SST, the share of variation in y explained by the regressors; 0 <= R^2 <= 1 with an intercept. R^2 never decreases when regressors are added, so adjusted R^2 = 1 - (SSR/(n-k))/(SST/(n-1)) penalises extra parameters. A high R^2 does not imply causation, correct specification or good out-of-sample fit; a low R^2 is common and acceptable in cross-sections.

## Blog post: Diagnostic testing in linear regression

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/diagnostic-testing-linear-regression.html
Content: testing the OLS assumptions. Heteroskedasticity: White test and Breusch-Pagan test (regress squared residuals on regressors; T*R^2 ~ chi^2 under H0), remedy with heteroskedasticity-consistent (White/HC) standard errors. Serial correlation: Durbin-Watson and Breusch-Godfrey LM test, remedy with HAC (Newey-West) standard errors. Functional form: Ramsey RESET test. Normality of residuals: Jarque-Bera test. Diagnostics affect efficiency and the validity of standard errors rather than the unbiasedness of the coefficients (except specification error).

## Blog post: Autocorrelation and serial correlation

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/autocorrelation-serial-correlation.html
Content: serial correlation is correlation between regression errors at different times, Cov(u_t, u_s) != 0, common in time series. It leaves OLS coefficients unbiased but makes ordinary standard errors wrong (usually too small), so t and F tests are invalid. Detect with the residual correlogram, Durbin-Watson (for AR(1)) and the Breusch-Godfrey test. Remedies: HAC (Newey-West) standard errors, or modelling the dynamics directly (e.g. adding lags / an ARMA error structure).

## Blog post: Instrumental variables and weak instruments

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/instrumental-variables-weak-instruments.html
Content: when a regressor is endogenous, E[x*u] != 0, OLS is biased and inconsistent (from simultaneity, omitted variables or measurement error). An instrument z must satisfy relevance Cov(z,x) != 0 and exogeneity / exclusion Cov(z,u)=0. IV estimator beta_IV = (Z'X)^{-1} Z'y; with more instruments than endogenous regressors, use 2SLS. Weak instruments (Cov(z,x) near zero) bias 2SLS toward OLS and invalidate standard inference; diagnose with the first-stage F (rule of thumb F > 10; Stock-Yogo critical values are stricter). Identification-robust options: Anderson-Rubin test, LIML, conditional likelihood ratio.

## Blog post: Generalized Method of Moments (GMM)

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/generalized-method-of-moments-gmm.html
Content: GMM estimates parameters from population moment conditions E[g(w, theta_0)] = 0 by minimising the quadratic form g_n(theta)' W g_n(theta) for a weighting matrix W. With more moments than parameters the model is over-identified; the efficient choice of W is the inverse of the variance of the moments, implemented as two-step (or iterated / continuously-updated) GMM. OLS, IV and 2SLS are special cases. Over-identifying restrictions are tested with Hansen's J statistic, J ~ chi^2(number of over-identifying restrictions) under correct specification.

## Blog post: Identification-robust inference with singular variance

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/identification-robust-inference-singular-variance.html
Content: identification-robust inference (Anderson-Rubin type) builds confidence regions that stay valid even when instruments are weak or parameters are weakly identified, by inverting a test of the moment conditions rather than relying on a point estimate. This article connects to Dr Grant's own research on the generalised Anderson-Rubin (GAR) statistic when the moment variance matrix is singular, where the standard chi-squared limit can fail without a first-order moment-singularity condition (Grant and Smith).

## Blog post: Confidence intervals and t-tests

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/confidence-intervals-t-tests.html
Content: a confidence interval is a range that contains the true parameter in a fixed proportion (e.g. 95%) of repeated samples. For a mean with unknown variance, CI = xbar +/- t_{n-1, alpha/2} * s/sqrt(n); with known variance use z. A two-sided t-test rejects H0: theta = theta_0 at level alpha exactly when theta_0 falls outside the (1-alpha) confidence interval, so the two are equivalent. Confidence is a property of the procedure, not a probability statement about one particular interval.

## Blog post: Distributions in econometrics (normal, t, chi-squared, F)

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/distributions-econometrics-normal-t-chi-f.html
Content: the four core sampling distributions. Normal: limit of sample means via the CLT and the basis of asymptotic inference. Chi-squared: sum of squared independent standard normals, used for variance and Wald/LM tests. Student-t: standard normal divided by sqrt(chi^2/df), used for coefficient tests when the error variance is estimated; approaches the normal as df grows. F: ratio of two independent chi-squared variables each over their df, used for joint hypotheses (e.g. testing several coefficients at once). Relationships: t^2 = F(1, df); chi^2/df -> F as the denominator df grows.

## Blog post: Maximum likelihood estimation explained

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/maximum-likelihood-estimation-explained.html
Content: MLE chooses theta to maximise the (log-)likelihood logL(theta) = sum log f(y_i; theta), the parameter value that makes the observed data most probable. Under regularity conditions the MLE is consistent, asymptotically normal and asymptotically efficient (attains the Cramer-Rao bound), with variance given by the inverse Fisher information. Used throughout econometrics for ARMA, GARCH, logit/probit and other non-linear models; nested models are compared with the likelihood ratio test and order is chosen with AIC/BIC.

## Blog post: ARMA estimation and model selection

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/arma-estimation-model-selection.html
Content: estimating ARMA(p,q) models. AR terms can be estimated by OLS; MA and mixed ARMA terms require maximum likelihood because the errors are unobserved. Order selection uses the ACF/PACF (the ACF cuts off after q for MA(q), the PACF cuts off after p for AR(p)) together with information criteria AIC = -2logL + 2K and BIC = -2logL + K*log(T), where BIC penalises complexity more heavily. Residuals should resemble white noise, checked with the Ljung-Box Q test.

## Blog post: Vector autoregression (VAR) basics

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/vector-autoregression-var-basics.html
Content: a VAR models several time series jointly, each variable regressed on its own and the others' lags: Y_t = c + A_1 Y_{t-1} + ... + A_p Y_{t-p} + e_t, with e_t vector white noise. A VAR(p) is stationary when the roots of the characteristic polynomial lie outside the unit circle (equivalently the companion matrix eigenvalues lie inside it). Used for forecasting and dynamic analysis through impulse response functions (the effect of a shock over time), forecast error variance decomposition, and Granger causality tests.

## Blog post: Modelling the distribution of stock returns

URL: https://www.drnickygrant.com/blog/modelling-stock-returns-distribution.html
Content: returns are usually computed as simple returns R_t = (P_t - P_{t-1})/P_{t-1} or log returns r_t = log(P_t) - log(P_{t-1}). Empirically, returns are roughly mean-zero over short horizons, show little autocorrelation in the level but strong dependence in squared/absolute returns (volatility clustering), and have fat tails / excess kurtosis (leptokurtosis) and mild negative skew. The normal distribution understates tail risk, so heavier-tailed alternatives (Student-t, generalised error distribution) and conditional-variance models (ARCH/GARCH) are used instead.

### GAR Singularity 2025 (most recent, with Richard J. Smith, Cambridge)
File: /research/tex/GAR-Singularity-2025-Grant-Smith.tex
Authors: Nicky L. Grant (University of St Andrews), Richard J. Smith (University of Cambridge)
Title: Generalised Anderson-Rubin Statistic Based Inference In The Presence Of A Singular Moment Variance Matrix
Status: Working paper, 2025
JEL: C13, C31, C51, C52
Keywords: Laurent series expansion; moment indicator; parameter sequences; singular moment matrix

Abstract: The paper constructs a confidence region with pointwise asymptotically correct size for the true value of a parameter of interest based on the generalized Anderson-Rubin (GAR) statistic when the moment variance matrix Omega is singular. The large-sample behaviour of the GAR statistic T_n(theta) = n * g_n(theta)' * Omega_n(theta)^{-1} * g_n(theta) is analysed using a Laurent series expansion around the points of moment variance singularity. Under a condition termed first-order moment singularity the GAR statistic is shown to possess a limiting chi-square distribution on parameter sequences converging to the true parameter value. Violation of this condition renders the GAR statistic unbounded asymptotically. The paper details an appropriate discretisation of the parameter space to implement a feasible GAR-based confidence region that contains the true parameter value with pointwise asymptotically correct size. No regularisation or pre-testing is required; all points of singularity need not be known in advance. Direct extension of Stock and Wright (2000) to the singular Omega case.

Key sections: Introduction; Notation; Asymptotic Properties of GAR Statistic with Singular Moment Variance; Feasible GAR-Based Confidence Regions; Conditions for N(Omega) ⊆ N(G'); Simulation Evidence; Summary and Conclusions; Appendix A (Auxiliary Lemmas for Laurent expansion); Appendix B (Proofs of main theorems).

### GMM Singular Variance Working Paper (Grant, 2018)
File: /research/tex/GMM-Singular-Variance-Grant-2018.tex
Title: GMM Estimation and Inference with (Asymptotically) Singular Moment Variance
Author: Nicky L. Grant (University of Manchester)
Date: September 2018
Keywords: GMM, Efficiency, Singular Variance

Abstract: Studies efficient 2-Step GMM estimation from strongly identified linear moment functions with (asymptotically) singular moment variance at the true parameter beta_0. The 2-Step GMM estimator with singular variance converges at rate sqrt(n) to a non-standard limit distribution, with convergence at rate n in certain directions when the null space of the moment variance matrix does not lie within that of the outer product of the expected first order derivative at beta_0. ## COMMON QUESTIONS

Q: Do you need Further Maths for economics at Cambridge?
A: A Level Mathematics is required and Further Mathematics is strongly encouraged for Cambridge Economics. Most successful applicants have Further Maths. If unavailable at your school, demonstrate mathematical ability via a strong TMUA score.

Q: What TMUA score is needed for Cambridge economics?
A: No fixed cut-off, but competitive applicants typically score above 6.5, strong candidates above 7.0, on the 1.0-9.0 scale. The test is learnable with targeted preparation.

Q: Difference between economics and econometrics?
A: Economics studies resource allocation (micro, macro, theory). Econometrics applies statistical methods to economic data to estimate relationships, test theories, and forecast. Econometrics is a quantitative subfield of economics.

Q: General maths tutor or econometrics specialist?
A: A general maths tutor suffices for GCSE/A Level maths foundations and introductory statistics. For econometrics (OLS assumptions, IV, GMM, time series, identification), a specialist is far more effective because the subject blends statistical theory with economic interpretation. The gap widens at university and postgraduate level.

Q: How long is a Cambridge economics interview?
A: Typically 20-45 minutes, usually one or two interviews. They are problem-solving conversations, not knowledge tests. Thinking aloud and responding well to challenge matters most.

Q: What is OLS?
A: Ordinary Least Squares fits a line through data by minimising the sum of squared residuals. It estimates how a dependent variable changes on average per one-unit change in an explanatory variable. The foundational estimation method in econometrics.

Q: What is the Gauss-Markov theorem?
A: Under classical linear model assumptions, OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE): smallest variance among all linear unbiased estimators.

Q: What is heteroskedasticity and does it matter?
A: Non-constant error variance across observations. It does not bias OLS coefficients but makes standard errors wrong, so use heteroskedasticity-robust (White) standard errors.

Q: What is autocorrelation?
A: Correlated regression errors across observations, common in time series. Does not bias coefficients but invalidates ordinary standard errors; use Newey-West (HAC) standard errors.

Q: What is stationarity?
A: A time series is weakly stationary if mean, variance are constant over time and autocovariance depends only on the lag. Required for standard time series inference; non-stationary data risks spurious regression.

Q: What is a p-value?
A: The probability of observing data at least as extreme as observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true. NOT the probability the null is true. Small p-value = evidence against the null. Statistical significance is not practical importance.

Q: What is GARCH for?
A: Modelling time-varying volatility in financial data, capturing volatility clustering (large moves follow large moves). Used in risk management, option pricing, volatility forecasting.

Q: Is an agency or independent tutor better?
A: Agency is convenient and fine for GCSE/standard A Level. Independent specialist is better value and quality for advanced/specialised subjects (university econometrics) with no markup and direct verification of expertise.
